      <p class="g-body">Some residents in the area have looked on the store as a stain on the community that should be razed and forgotten. Others have said it should be restored as a tribute to Emmett and a reminder of the hate that took his life.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">As the debate has played out over the decades, the store has continued to deteriorate and collapse, even amid frequent cultural and racial reckonings across the nation on the fate of Confederate monuments. At stake in Money and other communities across the country is the question of how Americans choose to acknowledge the country’s past.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">“It’s part of this bigger story, part of a history that we can learn from,” said the Rev. Wheeler Parker, 79, a pastor in suburban Chicago and a cousin of Emmett’s who went with him to Bryant’s Grocery that day. “The store should be one of the places we share Emmett’s story.”</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">(The Justice Department <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2018/07/12/us/emmett-till-death-investigation.html">quietly reopened the Emmett Till case</a> last year after Carolyn Bryant Donham, the white shopkeeper, recanted parts of her story.)</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">In and around the Delta, the memory of Emmett’s murder lingers.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">The cotton gin from which the 75-pound fan that was tethered to his neck with barbed wire was stolen is now a small museum. There are informal tours of the abandoned bridge where his body was likely tossed into the river. The barn where he was brutally beaten is unmarked, but its owner allows the occasional visitor.</p>



    </div>

    </div>

      <h2>‘Tear Off the Scab’</h2>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">Willie Williams and Donna Spell grew up about eight miles from each other in the Delta. They are 10 years apart in age. He learned about Emmett Till as a child. She learned about him as an adult. Mr. Williams is black. Ms. Spell is white.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">Mr. Williams said his parents told him about Emmett’s story “as a way of being careful.” Ms. Spell said Emmett’s horrific death was not a story “my parents would have told their children.”</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">

    </div>

    </div>

      <h2>Confronting History</h2>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">Susan Glisson has worked with a half-dozen Mississippi towns on racial healing, including in Sumner with the Emmett Till Memorial Commission. After she retired as director of the University of Mississippi’s William Winter Institute for Racial Reconciliation, she founded Sustainable Equity, a consulting firm focused on facilitating racial dialogue at universities, police departments, corporations and municipalities.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">“When it works, we are able to get past the perspective of ‘I didn’t do it, I don’t know anybody that did it,’ and find the ways to honor the victims,” Ms. Glisson said.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">When it doesn’t work, she went on, the resistance is stark: communities fracture, landmarks are neglected, significant events are lost or forgotten. These moments of tension and reckoning have buckled across America as small towns confront their racist histories.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">In northwest Florida, an all-black town was wiped off the map by racial violence during the Rosewood massacre in 1923. The one house that survived — where black residents hid to escape the slaughter — is now owned by an 85-year-old Japanese widow, Fujiko Scoggins. Her daughter and son-in-law, both real estate agents, are selling the home.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-ad ">
    

      

      <div class="g-ad-wrapper g-ad-" data-placement-name=""></div>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">A small heritage group wants to convert it into a Rosewood museum and garden, but hasn’t secured funding. Neighbors warned Ms. Scoggins’s son-in-law not to sell the house to black buyers, presumably to stop any commemoration of the massacre.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">The historical marker and road sign have been repeatedly vandalized. “The message is they don’t want Rosewood or the massacre to be remembered,” said Sherry Dupree, founder of the Rosewood Heritage Foundation and a tour guide.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">In Monroe, Ga., a racially violent chapter is commemorated annually. Two African-American married couples were murdered by a white mob near the Moore’s Ford Bridge, after a dispute with a farmer in 1946.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">Since 2005, a group of actors and activists have gathered each year to re-enact what happened that July night. “The people in town pretty much ignore it now every year,” said Cassandra Greene, who directs the performances. “But it’s important to keep doing it as a reminder of racial injustices.”</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">Memorials have the power to invite meaningful race conversations, Ms. Glisson added, but the key is addressing stubborn attitudes, stereotypes and assumptions that have been hardened and passed down over generations. The difficulty is getting beyond feelings of recrimination and guilt.</p>

      <h2>‘It’s Been Complicated’</h2>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">The price of Bryant’s Grocery & Meat Market, according to one Mississippi newspaper, is $4 million, but it’s hard to know more because the family has largely refused to talk publicly about it. Numerous messages and emails sent to the Tribbles for this story went unreturned.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">In 2011, the family was awarded a $206,000 state civil rights grant to restore a gas station next to the store. At the time, the project’s architect described the store restoration as the next phase. Since 2015, Mr. Weems has negotiated with family members, to no avail.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">There’s talk in town of a replica being built on state property across the street by one of the production companies filming movies about the Emmett Till case. That may be the only solution.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">“It’s been complicated working with the family,” Mr. Weems said. “We have had off and on discussions with the Tribbles for about three years and it seems as if every time we get close, they move the goal post.</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">“And I still don’t know what they want,” he added. “I don’t know if it’s money or they want control of the story that’s told, which has direct legacy implications for their father. I am hopeful that one day they can see a positive legacy by reclaiming the past.”</p>



    </div>

    

    
      <div class="g-item  g-item-body  g-text ">
    

      

      <p class="g-body">Today, fewer than 100 people live in Money and most of the property, including the old Bryant’s Grocery store, is owned by the children of Ray Tribble.</p>
